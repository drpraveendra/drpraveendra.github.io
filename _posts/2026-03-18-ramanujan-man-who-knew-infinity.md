Few stories in the history of mathematics are as remarkable as that of **Srinivasa Ramanujan** (1887–1920) — a self-taught genius from a small town in South India who, with almost no formal training, produced results that stunned the greatest mathematicians of his era.

---

## Early Life — A Spark in Kumbakonam

Ramanujan was born on December 22, 1887, in **Erode, Tamil Nadu**, and grew up in **Kumbakonam**. From childhood, his obsession with numbers was evident. By age 11 he had mastered the mathematical knowledge of two college students who lodged at his home. By 13, he had worked through **S. L. Loney's Trigonometry** entirely on his own.

The turning point came at age 15 when he obtained a copy of **G. S. Carr's "A Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics"** — a dry, dense collection of 5000 theorems with almost no proofs. Rather than being discouraged, Ramanujan used it as a launching pad, independently verifying and then vastly extending each result.

---

## The Notebooks — Mathematics Written in Isolation

Without access to journals, professors, or a research environment, Ramanujan filled three notebooks with thousands of results in number theory, infinite series, continued fractions, and elliptic functions. He worked largely in isolation, often unable to explain *how* he obtained his results.

> *"An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God."*
> — Srinivasa Ramanujan

---

## The Letter That Changed Everything

In January 1913, Ramanujan wrote a letter to **G. H. Hardy**, a leading mathematician at Cambridge University, containing over 120 theorems with no proofs. Hardy and his colleague **J. E. Littlewood** were astonished. Hardy later wrote:

> *"I had never seen anything in the least like them before. A single look at them is enough to show that they could only be written down by a mathematician of the highest class."*

Hardy arranged for Ramanujan to come to Cambridge, beginning one of the most celebrated collaborations in mathematical history.

---

## Five Years at Cambridge (1914–1919)

At Cambridge, Ramanujan produced an extraordinary body of work alongside Hardy. Together they developed the **Hardy–Ramanujan asymptotic formula** for the partition function $p(n)$:

$$p(n) \sim \frac{1}{4n\sqrt{3}} \exp\!\left(\pi\sqrt{\frac{2n}{3}}\right) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

In 1918, Ramanujan became the **first Indian to be elected a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS)**.

---

## Ramanujan's Most Famous Identity

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9801} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(4k)!(1103 + 26390k)}{(k!)^4 \cdot 396^{4k}}$$

Each term of this formula adds roughly **8 correct decimal digits** of $\pi$. It formed the basis for modern computer algorithms to calculate $\pi$ to trillions of digits.

---

## The Taxi Cab Number

Hardy visited Ramanujan in hospital and mentioned his taxi number was **1729**, calling it rather dull. Ramanujan immediately replied:

> *"No Hardy, it is a very interesting number! It is the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two different ways."*

$$1729 = 1^3 + 12^3 = 9^3 + 10^3$$

Such numbers are now called **taxicab numbers** in his honour.

---

## Death and Legacy

Ramanujan died on April 26, 1920, aged just **32 years**. In his brief life he produced approximately **3900 results** — the majority of which have since been proven correct. His **Lost Notebook**, discovered in 1976 at the Wren Library Cambridge, contained hundreds of additional results that kept mathematicians busy for decades.
